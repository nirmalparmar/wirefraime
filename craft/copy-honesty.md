# Copy & content honesty craft rules

How to write the words in a UI so it reads like a real product, not a
template. Especially load-bearing for landing/marketing pages, where copy
*is* the design.

## Honesty (linted partner: anti-ai-slop sins 5 & 6)

- **No invented metrics.** "10× faster", "99.9% uptime", "Join 50,000+
  teams", "4.9★ from 2,000 reviews" — unless grounded in a real source.
  When the number is unknown, label it: "Sample metric", "Pending", or
  drop the claim and make the section work on composition.
- **No filler.** Never `lorem ipsum`, "Feature one / two / three", "Card
  title", "Your headline here", "$XX.XX", "User 1". An empty slot is a
  layout problem, not a reason to type noise.
- **Honest placeholders for unknown data.** Mark sample/pending/
  unavailable explicitly instead of fabricating authority.

## Specificity beats generic

- Real names (Sarah Chen, Marcus Williams, Ada Patel), real prices
  ($4,280.50), real dates (Mar 15, 2026), realistic density.
- Microcopy that names the actual job: **"Start tracking"** > "Get
  started"; **"Create your first invoice"** > "Continue"; **"3 seats
  left on Pro"** > "Limited time".
- Button labels are verbs for what happens next, not "Submit"/"OK".

## Landing-page voice

- **Headline** states the outcome the user gets, not what the product is.
  "Ship your changelog in minutes" > "A changelog tool".
- **Subhead** earns the headline with one concrete specific, then stops.
- One **value per section** — don't stack three promises in one block.
- Social proof is specific or absent — a real-sounding quote with a name
  and role beats five logo-shaped grey boxes.
- The CTA repeats the core verb; don't switch from "Start tracking" up top
  to a vague "Sign up" at the bottom.

## Tone matches the brand

Read the design system's intended mood (calm enterprise, playful, luxury,
brutalist) and write to it. A calm B2B tool doesn't shout; a consumer app
can. Consistency of voice across sections is itself a craft signal.

## Common mistakes (reviewed)

- Fabricated stats presented as fact.
- "Get started" / "Learn more" / "Submit" as every button.
- Headline describes the product category instead of the user's outcome.
- Three competing value props crammed into one hero.
- Logo-soup social proof with no real testimonial.
